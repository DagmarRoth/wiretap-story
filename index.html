The Cost of Surveillance

How the U.S. Government Spends on Wiretaps

A three-year analysis reveals a troubling paradox: fewer wiretaps, but mounting costs

Scroll to explore

A $405 Million Question

In 2024, the U.S. government spent approximately $405 million on court-authorized wiretap operations—electronic surveillance of phone calls and communications.

This represented a 28% increase from 2022, despite the number of wiretaps actually declining by 5%.

Each wiretap operation now costs an average of $234,272—more than double the average car price.

The Three-Year Trend

As you scroll, watch how costs and orders diverge

2022: The Baseline Year

In 2022, the U.S. authorized 2,414 wiretap operations, averaging $180,847 per order. This was the baseline—seemingly sustainable.

2023: Costs Begin Rising

By 2023, costs increased 12.5% to $203,490 per order, while total orders declined slightly to 2,398. The divergence begins.

2024: The Breaking Point

In 2024, costs jumped another 15% to $234,272, while orders fell further to 2,297. For the first time, we're spending more while doing less.

Why?

Possibilities include: more sophisticated investigations, updated technology, longer operation durations, or more intensive resource allocation per case. The data doesn't tell us the reason—only the outcome.

The Effectiveness Problem

But how much value are we getting for this investment?

Only 1 in 9 recorded communications contains useful evidence

88.7% Waste

In 2024, wiretap operations recorded 2,448 total intercepts. Of these, only 277 were incriminating—containing evidence relevant to criminal investigation.

That means 88.7% of recorded communications were not incriminating. They captured the wrong numbers, innocent conversations, or irrelevant chatter.

Cost per incriminating intercept: $1.46 million

This raises fundamental questions about proportionality: Does recording millions of innocent communications justify the $405 million annual investment?

Half the Surveillance

California and New York combined account for 50% of all state-level wiretap orders.

Meanwhile, 25 states reported zero wiretap activity in 2024—despite having the legal authority to conduct them.

This raises questions about equity: Are some parts of America under far greater surveillance than others?

The Federal Dominance

56%

Federal wiretaps (1,290 orders) vs State (1,007 orders)

The federal government—FBI, DEA, Secret Service—accounts for 56% of all wiretap orders. Federal prosecutors have resources that most state systems simply cannot match.

This suggests that wiretap surveillance in America is largely a tool of federal law enforcement. Local police rarely have the capability or expertise to deploy them.

Questions Without Answers

The data tells us what happened. But it can't tell us why, or whether it was worth it.

Why are costs rising?

Technology upgrades? Longer investigations? More sophisticated criminals? More resources per case? The 29.5% cost increase demands explanation.

Why such low hit rates?

88.7% of intercepts are not incriminating. Is wiretapping inherently inefficient? Are investigators casting too wide a net?

Why the geographic gap?

California and New York account for half of state operations. Is this a reflection of actual crime rates, or resource distribution?

Is $405 million worth it?

For the cost of one year's wiretaps, we could fund new police academies, crime prevention, addiction treatment, or countless other interventions. What's the tradeoff?
