The Cost of Surveillance

How America's most expensive law enforcement tool is getting less effective while costing more.

The Paradox

In 2024, the United States government authorized 2,297 wiretap operations. The cost: $405 million.

Yet only 11.3% of the 2,448 intercepts recorded contained evidence relevant to criminal investigation.

This is not a one-year problem. It's a three-year deterioration. Costs are rising. Effectiveness is falling. And the trend is accelerating.

Two Lines, Two Stories

Watch what happens when you overlay cost and effectiveness. They tell opposite stories.

Red line (left axis): Average cost per order. Green line (right axis): Incrimination rate. Notice they cross and move in opposite directions.

Getting More Expensive, Less Effective

From 2022 to 2024, costs rose 29.5%. Effectiveness fell 12.4%. This is not coincidence. This is the core problem.

When you're spending more and getting less, the system is broken. It's not a question of resources or deployment. It's a question of fundamental viability.

The question is not whether costs will continue rising. The question is whether we should allow it.

Where Every Dollar Actually Goes

Green = evidence-producing. Red = recording innocent conversations. Watch how red grows.

In 2024, $258 million (63.7%) was spent recording people who had done nothing wrong. It literally was waste.

More Than Just Numbers

The composition chart above shows something troubling: the amount of money spent on non-incriminating intercepts is growing, not shrinking.

In 2022, 36.4% of spending went to evidence-producing operations. By 2024, that fell to 36.3%. Nearly identical. But with higher overall spending, the absolute waste grew to nearly $260 million.

This suggests the problem isn't operational inefficiency that can be fixed. It's structural. Wiretaps may simply be inherently inefficient.

The Cost-Effectiveness Problem

Each bubble is a year. Size = number of wiretap orders. Notice the relationship: more expensive, less effective.

Ideal would be upper-left (cheap + effective). We're moving toward upper-right (expensive + ineffective).

Not All States Are Equal

Wiretap authority exists in 50 states. But wiretap usage is concentrated.

California: 366 orders (36% of all state operations). New York: 138. Nevada: 93. Everyone else combined: 450.

This creates a two-tier system: some states have intensive electronic surveillance infrastructure, others barely use it at all.

The Leading States: Cost, Volume, and Effectiveness

Each bubble is a state (2024 data). X-axis = average cost per order. Y-axis = effectiveness rate. Size = number of orders.

Top states are spending differently and getting different results. California and New York dominate by volume, but Nevada and Colorado have higher effectiveness rates (though still low).

Federal Dominance

56% of wiretaps are federal (FBI, DEA, Secret Service). 44% are state and local.

This reveals a structural truth: electronic surveillance is primarily a tool of federal law enforcement. States and localities use wiretaps, but they don't have the same resources or expertise.

The system is built by and for the federal government.

The Federal-State Split

How wiretap authorizations break down: federal (blue) vs. state/local (orange). Watch the proportions.

Despite state authority in all 50 states, federal operations account for 56% of the total. States authorize wiretaps, but the feds dominate usage.
