The Cost of Surveillance

How America's most expensive law enforcement tool is getting less effective while costing more.

The Paradox

In 2024, the United States government authorized 2,297 wiretap operations. The cost: $405 million.

Yet only 11.3% of the 2,448 intercepts recorded contained evidence relevant to criminal investigation.

This is not a problem of a single year. It's a deteriorating trend. And we have three years of complete data to prove it.

What the Numbers Tell Us

The data above shows something alarming: every single metric is moving in the wrong direction.

Costs are rising. Orders are declining. Effectiveness is plummeting. The cost per result has more than doubled in three years.

But raw numbers can hide as much as they reveal. To truly understand what's happening, we need to explore the data from multiple angles.

Three Years of Divergence

2022 was the baseline. Expensive, but perhaps justifiable. The system seemed stable.

2023 was a warning. Costs jumped 12.5%. Effectiveness barely budged.

2024 was the breaking point. Costs jumped another 15.1%. Effectiveness collapsed to 11.3%.

The acceleration is the story. This isn't a stable system reaching equilibrium. This is a system spiraling.

Not All States Are Equal

Wiretap authority is spread across 50 states. But wiretap usage is concentrated in a handful.

California alone accounts for 36% of all state operations. California and New York combined: 50%.

This disparity raises fundamental questions about surveillance equity and resource allocation.

The Unsustainable Trajectory

The data above is comprehensive, and it tells a clear story: American wiretap operations are becoming less efficient while consuming more resources.

This is not a stable system. This is not a mature technology. This is a tool that is failing at its core mission while requiring ever-greater investment.

The question is not whether this trend will reverse. The question is whether we will allow it to continue.

The Questions We Must Ask

The data is complete. The evidence is overwhelming. Now comes the hard part: deciding what to do about it.

Why are costs accelerating?

The cost increase from 2023 to 2024 (15.1%) was larger than from 2022 to 2023 (12.5%). Why is this trend worsening, not stabilizing?

Why is effectiveness declining?

With an 11.3% incrimination rate, are we deploying wiretaps correctly? Are the targets sophisticated criminals avoiding detection? Or is the tool itself fundamentally limited?

Is this proportional?

$1.46 million per incriminating intercept. Can any law enforcement technique justify this cost-to-result ratio?

Is this equitable?

California and New York benefit from intensive surveillance infrastructure. Most states barely use wiretaps. Is electronic surveillance a tool for everyone, or only for the wealthy?

The data has been presented. The visualization has been completed. The numbers speak. Now it is time to listen.
