The federal government spent $405 million on wiretap operations in 2024. Yet only 11.3% of recorded communications contained evidence relevant to criminal investigation. That means 88.7% of the money—approximately $359 million—went toward recording innocent conversations.

We're paying more to get worse results.

Visual 2: Where the Money Actually Goes

Breaking down the $405 million annual expenditure

The Total Investment

In 2024, America spent $405 million on court-authorized wiretap operations. This represents every dollar of government resources dedicated to electronic surveillance.

The Effective Portion

Of those 2,448 total intercepts, only 277 were incriminating. That's $147 million spent on wiretaps that produced evidence.

36.3% of the budget yielded results.

The Wasted Portion

The remaining $258 million was spent recording innocent communications: wrong numbers, unrelated conversations, personal matters with no criminal relevance.

63.7% of the budget produced nothing useful.

The Real Cost Per Result

To get one incriminating intercept, the government spent $1.46 million. That's more than a house, more than a luxury car, more than a decade of an officer's salary.

For one piece of evidence.

The Acceleration Problem

The cost increase is accelerating. From 2022 to 2023, costs rose 12.5%. From 2023 to 2024, they jumped 15.1%. If this trend continues:

  • 2025 projected: ~$270K per order
  • 2026 projected: ~$311K per order

🚨 Every Metric Points Downward

This is a rare situation where all six key metrics move in the wrong direction simultaneously:

  • Costs ↑ (three different cost measures all rising)
  • Effectiveness ↓ (incrimination rate falling)
  • Efficiency ↓ (cost per result soaring)

There is no way to spin this as positive. The system is getting more expensive and less effective.

The Unanswered Questions

Why is cost accelerating?

The 15.1% increase from 2023-2024 is faster than the 12.5% increase from 2022-2023. Why are individual operations becoming progressively more expensive?

Why is effectiveness declining?

With 88.7% of intercepts being non-incriminating, are investigators casting nets too wide? Or are sophisticated criminals simply avoiding recorded communication?

Is this proportional?

$1.46 million per incriminating intercept. Does any law enforcement technique justify this cost-to-result ratio? Could the money be better spent?

Where is the oversight?

With courts approving 99.4% of wiretap requests and costs continuously rising, what mechanism exists to question whether wiretaps are the right tool?

The Data is Clear

The U.S. is spending more than ever on wiretaps while getting worse results. Whether this represents a failure of the surveillance technique itself, a failure of how it's being deployed, or a failure of oversight, the trend is unsustainable and unacceptable.

Without intervention, these numbers will only worsen.
