THE COST-EFFECTIVENESS COLLAPSE

From 2022 to 2024, the cost per wiretap order rose 29.5%—from $180,847 to $234,272. Over that same period, the incrimination rate fell from 12.9% to 11.3%. Source: Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts, Wiretap Report, 2024 The two trend lines cross and diverge, charting a system in freefall.

This divergence is not an anomaly. It is the visible trace of systemic rot. As agencies pour more resources into surveillance infrastructure—equipment upgrades, personnel costs, facility maintenance— the returns diminish. Courts reject tainted evidence. Suspects walk free. Taxpayers foot a bill for negligence disguised as necessity.

Wasted intercepts don't fall randomly. They cluster in high-population jurisdictions where federal task forces, state police, and local law enforcement step on each other's toes. // EDITORIAL — THE RECKONING

OVERSIGHT FAILURE. DEMOCRATIC EROSION.

The wiretap system represents a fundamental failure of democratic oversight. Congress authorizes spending. Agencies spend it. Courts rubber-stamp applications. But no one asks: Is this returning results? Is it necessary? Is this worth the cost—financial or constitutional?

The $258 million in waste is not a rounding error. It is evidence of a system that has lost its moorings. When costs rise 30% while effectiveness falls, when the same suspects are tapped by overlapping agencies, when 35% of captured calls are personal in nature and legally irrelevant, the problem is not marginal. The problem is systemic.

The path forward demands action: Consolidate federal-state wiretap coordination to eliminate redundancy. Establish enforceable accuracy standards for intercept applications. Mandate real-time judicial review of target selection. Require agencies to justify sustained surveillance when effectiveness metrics decline. Create an independent oversight body with subpoena power. Establish sunset clauses—taps expire unless reauthorized and justified quarterly.

These are not radical proposals. They are basic democratic hygiene. Yet they remain absent from federal statute. The wiretap system operates in the dark, accountable to no one, corrected by no one, questioned by almost no one. That must change. The numbers demand it. Democracy requires it.
