An Investigation

The Cost of Surveillance

Spending skyrockets. Effectiveness plummets. A three-year reckoning with America's most expensive law enforcement tool.

The Paradox

Every year, the United States government spends hundreds of millions of dollars on wiretaps—court-authorized electronic surveillance of phone calls and private communications. It is the most intrusive tool available to law enforcement, requiring a judge to find probable cause that a serious crime is being committed.

In 2024, America authorized 2,297 wiretap operations. The cost: $405 million.

Yet of all the communications recorded—2,448 total intercepts—only 277 contained evidence relevant to a criminal investigation.

That's an 11.3% success rate. Or, looking at it another way: 88.7% waste.

In 2024, wiretap spending reached $405 million despite declining authorization rates. Yet effectiveness metrics show deteriorating results.

Getting More Expensive, Less Effective

The trend is worsening. From 2022 to 2024, the average cost per wiretap operation increased by 29.5%—from $180,847 to $234,272.

That alone would be alarming. But here's what makes it a crisis: while costs rose, results fell.

In 2022, 12.9% of intercepts were incriminating. By 2024, that had declined to 11.3%. We're spending more money to catch fewer criminals.

The mathematicians among you will understand the real problem: the cost per incriminating intercept climbed 41.4%. From $1.03 million to $1.46 million.

Three lines, three stories. All pointing the wrong direction.
$258 Million
spent recording innocent conversations

Of the $405 million spent on wiretaps in 2024, $147 million funded operations that produced evidence. The rest—nearly $260 million—was spent recording people who had done nothing wrong.

People who weren't the target. People who picked up the phone at the wrong time. People whose only connection to a crime was being in the wrong place at the wrong moment.

All recorded. All listened to. All representing an investment that returned nothing.

Of every dollar spent on wiretaps, 63.7¢ produces no evidence.

Three Years of Deterioration

2022

The Baseline

2,414 orders authorized. Average cost: $180,847. Incrimination rate: 12.9%.

This is the starting point. Before the crisis.

2023

The Warning Sign

2,398 orders. Costs jumped 12.5% to $203,490. Incrimination rate: 12.6%.

Still manageable. The system appeared stable, even if expensive.

2024

The Breaking Point

2,297 orders. Costs surged another 15.1% to $234,272. Incrimination rate: 11.3%.

Now we have fewer operations, higher costs, and worse results. This is not sustainable.

The cost trajectory is accelerating, not stabilizing.

What Happened?

The data tells us what happened. It cannot tell us why.

Are wiretaps becoming more technologically sophisticated?

Newer surveillance methods, better equipment, more complex implementations—all would explain rising costs.

Are investigations becoming more complex?

Perhaps the cases that warrant wiretaps are inherently more complicated and resource-intensive than before.

Are criminals getting smarter?

Sophisticated criminal networks may avoid recorded communications, forcing law enforcement to cast wider nets and record more innocent people.

Is the entire tool fundamentally flawed?

With an 11.3% success rate, perhaps wiretapping is simply an inefficient investigative technique that should be deprioritized.

The data cannot answer these questions. But we must.

A System Built on Disparity

Wiretap authority is spread across all 50 states. Wiretap usage is concentrated in a handful.

California
366 orders (36.3% of all state operations)
New York
138 orders (13.7%)
All Other States
503 orders (49.9% combined)

California and New York—home to just 7% of the U.S. population—authorize 50% of all state-level wiretaps.

Meanwhile, 25 states reported zero wiretap activity in 2024, despite having the legal authority.

This isn't about crime rates. It's about resources, institutional knowledge, and prosecutorial choices.

Electronic surveillance in America is geographically concentrated. Some states embrace it. Others barely use it.

Federal Dominance

Of all wiretap operations in 2024, 56% were federal—FBI, DEA, Secret Service, and other federal agencies.

This reveals a deeper truth: wiretaps are primarily a tool of federal law enforcement. State and local police rarely have the resources or expertise to deploy them.

What appears to be a widespread investigative technique is actually dominated by a small number of highly resourced federal prosecutors. They have the technology, the personnel, the legal expertise.

Everyone else works with what they can afford.

All metrics trending the same direction: toward unsustainability.

The Questions We Must Ask

Is this proportional?

Can we justify $1.46 million per incriminating intercept? For perspective, that's more than a house, more than a car, more than a decade of a teacher's salary. For one piece of evidence.

Is this effective?

An 11.3% success rate. If you picked randomly, you'd get lucky more often. If this were any other law enforcement tool—police stops, search warrants, forensic testing—we'd be asking hard questions about ROI.

Is this equitable?

California and New York get intensive electronic surveillance. Montana and Vermont barely touch it. This isn't about need. It's about resources. It's about power.

Is this necessary?

With social media, digital forensics, metadata analysis, and traditional investigation techniques, do we still need wiretaps? Or are we continuing them because the infrastructure exists and the programs have institutional momentum?

The data doesn't answer these questions. But it demands that we ask them.
